Problem Solving

Hello Friend

Let me tell you about a time I got blocked on simple problem, but solved the problem in an elegant way.

Let me start off by making it clear that I don't believe for a second that my solution was elegant. In fact, far from it I'm sure. Could I be excused if I said that the problem was solved, and I just happened to be resplendent in a glorious, yet refined, ball gown at the time?

"I'm sure your solution was elegant, Andrew, don't be so hard on yourself" I hear you say. You'll just have to trust me, because I came across the solution after abandoning all hope that I'd be the one to solve it.

You probably know it

It was the Chess board challenge from the Eloquent JavaScript book.

I own that book. I've read that chapter twice before. The first time online, the second time was after I bought the book knowing full well that I struggle to read anything of length online. My mind is too ready to flutter off elsewhere. I've done the chess board exercise before, but I couldn't remember how I did it or how easy/hard I found it.

This time I found it HARD. Not how to code it so much as how to solve it. Pseudocode was no use to me because I couldn't nut it out (without having two long strings of spaces and checkers in an if/else statement. Trying something was a failure at the start because I didn't know where to. Loops within loops, I learned, after reluctantly resorting to the hidden exercise hints. Loops within loops was how to go about it. I find it gets pretty tough to keep track of with loops in loops.

I solved it. Inelegantly I'm sure, but it worked. I had to step through the loops with debugging, and console.log the guts out of the string I was building, but I found that whole exercise a bit of a kick in the joy. Everything was going so smoothly before that.

It's such a strange thing that happens in your mind when you expect to see a certain output from your code and you see something completely different. There's a real panic flavour to it. That question about this as a career choice returns. The self-doubt looms heavier. Isn't it funny how self-doubt seems so self-assured about itself? Self-doubt seems so much easier to believe than self-belief. Self-doubt is convinced it is fact. Self-believe feels like a wishlist that'll never come true. It's a hard thing to change about yourself, that's for sure.

I was asked about how I felt about other thus far unmentioned problem solving methods

The rubber ducky method is difficult for me because I'm all internal. One of the things I most hope to get out of this bootcamp is a sizable improvement in my code-communication skills. I've been doing it for so long alone that talking about it seems like a scary, unfamiliar man standing motionless in my yard in the small hours. Not something I want to approach, but something I know I have to. Anyway, didn't have any luck with the duck.

Reading error messages. I do that, of course when I encounter them. They're essential when I understand them. Infuriating when I don't.

Googling. I do plenty of that and on a regular basis too. We're lucky to be studying in a field where it's possible to do that effectively. But just when to google? That's what I struggle with. Do I solve it myself or google it straight away? Which one makes me a better developer? There's some middle ground between 'straight away' and 'after hours of impasse after impasse'. Finding that is the what counts. Also about google, I don't like it when I find a solution to my problem but I still don't understand it. What do I do? Copy the code chunk and throw it in my project? Suddenly I loose all intimacy with the build. I can't trust it anymore because I don't know entirely what's going on. Anyway, I google like a banshee.

Asking peers or coaches for help. Haven't tried that yet. Not sure if that's because of lack of people to ask, or lack of willingness to. I have a strong need to learn things myself. Something I have to be careful with, lest I miss opportunities to progress.

I have no doubt about the effectiveness of reflection, though I do have doubts about my ability to effectively reflect. Not only do I often forget to do it, I'm not even sure I'm doing it right when I do.

So you want to know about the map, filter and reduce methods huh?

Take a number, pal.

The map() method iterates through an array and processes them each item through a given function and returns a new array.

The filter() method iterates through an array and processes each item through a given function. A new array is returned with only the items that return true from the given function.

The reduce() method reduces the array to a single value by passing each item through a function where a calculation is performed. This method can take three arguments: An accumulator (to keep the total), currentValue (the item currently being acted on), and currentIndex (the index of the item currently being acted on).
